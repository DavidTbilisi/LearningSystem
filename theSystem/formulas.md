# Formula / Symbolic Encoding System

Encodes **Greek letters, operators, quantifiers, and structural positions**
into vivid imagery so formulas become memorable scenes, not strings of symbols.

The system has three layers:
1. **Atom layer** — one image per symbol (Greek letters, operators)
2. **Structure layer** — positional zones within a scene (up/down/left/right/center)
3. **Composition layer** — how to weave atoms into a formula-scene

---

## Layer 2 — Structural Zones

A formula's position carries meaning. Encode position by **placing the atom in
a spatial zone** of the scene.

### The 5 zones

```
                    ⬆ UP zone
                 (exponent, superscript)

   ⬅ LEFT zone      ⊙ CENTER zone      RIGHT zone ➡
 (coefficient,     (main operator,    (argument,
 bound, summand     verb of formula)   what it acts on)
 starting point)

                    ⬇ DOWN zone
              (subscript, index, bound)
```

### How to use zones

Imagine the **main scene character** in the **CENTER**. Then:

- **Anything in the exponent** → put it *floating above* the character (in the sky, on its head)
- **Anything in the subscript** → put it *under the character's feet* (ground, shoes, basement)
- **Anything the operator acts on** → put it *to the right* of the character
- **Anything modifying before** (coefficients, bounds) → put it *to the left*

### Worked example: Σ from i=1 to n of xᵢ²

Read positionally:
- Σ → Sigma stacker (CENTER — the main action)
- i=1 → 1 on the ground under the stacker (DOWN zone, start of index)
- n → n floating above the stacker (UP zone, end of index)
- xᵢ → the stacker is picking up boxes labeled xᵢ (RIGHT zone)
- ² → each box has a small "2" flag on top (exponent inside the right-zone item)

**Full scene:** *The Sigma stacker crane stands between a "1" marker on the ground and an "n" flag in the sky, picking up squared boxes labeled xᵢ from the right and stacking them into a pile.*

## Layer 3 — Composition

Two composition modes depending on the formula's shape:

### Mode A — Sequential (for horizontal, left-to-right formulas)

Read the formula left to right. Each atom becomes a mini-scene, linked by
**physical contact** (handing off, pointing, throwing) to the next atom.

Example: `f(x) = αx + β`

1. **f** → a fisherman (F = 8)
2. **(x)** → holding a bucket labeled x
3. **=** → the bucket pours into a machine
4. **α** → the alpha wolf operates the machine
5. **x** → another x-bucket appears
6. **+** → gets plugged together with
7. **β** → a beta tape

**Chain scene:** *Fisherman hands x-bucket to alpha wolf at the machine; wolf produces another x-bucket plugged into a beta tape.*

### Mode B — Structural (for vertical, fraction-heavy, indexed formulas)

Use the 5-zone layout. Place atoms in their zones around a central character.

Example: `σ² = E[(X - μ)²]`

- σ² → sigma shooter, with a "2" flag on his head (UP zone)
- = → a mirror
- E → royal scale (CENTER of right side)
- X → an X-marked person on the scale
- μ → a cow, also on the scale
- X − μ → the cow is being subtracted (yanked away) from the person
- ( )² → the whole scene has a "2" flag above

**Scene:** *Sigma shooter (with 2-flag) looks in mirror and sees: a royal scale holding the X-person, while a cow (μ) is being yanked away, with a 2-flag floating above the whole scene.*

### Mode C — Hybrid (most real formulas)

Use **structural mode for the outer shell** and **sequential mode for each zone's content**.

Example: Bayes' theorem `P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A) / P(B)`

- Outer: fraction → one scene on top of another
- Top (numerator): sequential chain of three dice scenes
- Bottom (denominator): one dice scene with B
- Entire outer scene: two dice tables stacked with a dividing line

## Encoding Procedure

When you face a new formula:

1. **Identify the main operator** — what's the verb of this formula? That's your CENTER.
2. **Identify the output** — where does the result go? Usually left of `=`.
3. **Identify the inputs** — what feeds in? Usually right side.
4. **Place indices/bounds in DOWN/UP zones** — anything subscript/superscript.
5. **Walk atoms into a scene** — sequential for flat formulas, structural for indexed ones.
6. **Sanity test** — close eyes, picture it, try to reconstruct the formula. If you can't regenerate the symbols, the scene is too loose.
7. **Anki card** — front: formula written out. Back: the scene + which atoms in which zones.

## Integration with Existing Systems

- **Major System digits** — any number inside a formula (coefficients, exponents) uses Major images. `x⁵³` → x with a "lame" (53) floating above.
- **PAO chains** — a multi-step derivation is a PAO chain, one scene per step.
- **NEDF** — named theorems (Bayes, CLT, Cauchy-Schwarz) also get NEDF encoding. The formula scene is the *essence slot* of the NEDF.
- **Heuristic Palace** — when stuck on a derivation, walk Room 3 (Plan) — especially "Simpler World" (solve smaller case) and "Invariant" (what doesn't change).
- **Mind Palace** — store derivations along walks. Each locus = one formula scene.

## Key Principles

1. **Atoms first, structure second.** Learn the atom library before trying to encode whole formulas.
2. **Greek letters have digit tags.** Use them when the letter is indexed — α₂ isn't just "alpha-two," it's the alpha wolf holding a swan (2).
3. **Operators are verbs.** Never encode an operator as a static symbol — it must be *doing something*.
4. **Zones are consistent.** Up = above the character, Down = below, always. Break this rule and retrieval breaks.
5. **Test by reconstruction.** A good encoding lets you regenerate the formula from the scene. If you can only recognize it, it's not strong enough.
